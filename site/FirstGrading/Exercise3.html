How to Assemble a Basic Computer Step-by-Step Guide


This set of instructions will help you assemble a basic computer capable of running most
modern software encountered by a casual user. Modern computers become more affordable when users
supplement their monetary investment with a few hours of effort. This computer build is very basic
and will be the minimum hardware necessary to have a functional system. After you have all of the
parts and materials needed, it will take you some time to assemble your computer using simple hand
tools available at your home.

Things You Need to Know Before Building a Computer

  • Anyone can build a computer with the right preparation and set of instructions.
  • Building a computer yourself ensures that you can meet your required preferences.
  • While deciding on which components to buy consider reasearching the individual parts or other's recommendations online.
  • Carefully consider the computer parts that you buy and make sure that all of them are compatible with each other.
  • Most components come with additional parts; some optional, some required for installation in your build. You need to organize them
    to not get confused.

Components needed for building the computer.

  • Workspace
  • Screwdrivers
  • Zipties & Scissors For organization
  • Computer Case
  • Central Processing Unit (CPU)
  • Motherboard
  • Memory (RAM)
  • Graphics processing unit (GPU) This is optional
  • Storage
  • Power supply unit (PSU)
  • Operating system(OS)

Here are some details about the list above.

    Central Processing Unit (CPU) also known as the processor, is basically the brain of your PC.
    When a computer program runs, it sends a list of instructions to the CPU. The CPU performs each instruction
    and sends signals to other components to let them know when they need to perform a task. Has cooling a fan
    included.

    • Core Count tells us how many tasks the computer can perform simultaneously.
    • Clock Speed tells us how quickly the computer can perform the task.

    Motherboard is the main circuit where everything is connected to. The CPU sits directly on top of the board
    and both must be compatible with each other. There are a selection of motherboards based on size. Depending on the
    case or the components the most common form factors are the Extended ATX, ATX, microATX, and the Mini-ITX.

    Memory or RAM is the PC's short-term memory. This is where the PC stores and access data that is currently active.
    More RAM means better performance but too little RAM is the opposite. To not waste money, you should spend depending on
    your budget, preference, and the compatibility to the motherboard.

    Graphics Processing Unit (GPU) is used in a wide range of applications, including graphics and video rendering.
    They’re best known for their capabilities in gaming.

    • Integrated Graphics are less powerful than discrete graphics but it is all you need for a basic build
      These processors are integrated with the CPU.
    • Discrete Graphics powerful components that plug into the motherboard via PCIe, and come with their own resources,
      including video memory and (usually) an active cooling system.

    Storage are devices where you can store long-term memory.

    • Solid State Drive (SSD) are fast and reliable flash memoryused in a USB drive to store data.
    • Hard Disk Drive (HDD) use mechanical arms, and a spinning platter to store and retrieve data.

    Operating System is a critical piece of software that assists in managing communications between a computer’s hardware and programs.

Getting Started

    Prepare the computer case

    There are different sizes of computer cases, make sure the components are compatible with the case and vice versa.

    Here are some details about the different sizes.

    • Full-tower cases are designed to fit both Extended-ATX motherboards and standard full-size
      ATX motherboards. They usually measure around 22-24 inches tall, 18-20 inches long, and more than 8 inches wide.
    • Mid-tower cases are designed to fit standard full-size ATX motherboards. Generally speaking, mid-tower is the
      most common case size. Their dimensions can vary quite a bit, but these cases usually measure around 18-20
      inches tall, 17-20 inches long, and 6-8 inches wide.
    • Mini-tower cases , or small form factor (SFF) builds, are compact and designed to fit a number of smaller
      motherboards, such as mini-ITX motherboards.

    After preparing all the things you need now you can start building by doing the following:

    1. Installing the CPU

      1. Unpack the CPU and the motherboard carefuly and find the cpu socket.
      2. Refer to the instructions that come with the motherboard and CPU.
      3. Remove the socket tray by sliding the lever to the side gently.
      4. Usually you align the small arrow on the CPU on the CPU socket on the motherboard.
      5. Once you correctly socketed the CPU, pull down the lever back to its original position.
      6. Here is an image for reference.

    2. Installing the storage M.2 SSD (Optional)

      1. First, find the M.2 slot on your motherboard. It's a small, horizontal slot with a tiny screw across from it.
      2. Remove the screw.
      3. Slide the M.2 SSD gently into the slot. When it's fully seated, it will stand off the motherboard about a 35-degree angle.
        Push the SSD down and replace the tiny screw to lock it in place.

    3. Installing the CPU cooling system

      1. Consult the instruction manual of the cooler for exact installation.
      2. The motherboard may have brackets installed. You may need it or you may not unless the cooler uses a different bracket.
      3. The coolers may have thermal paste or thermal pad pre-installed, if the cooler has none, you have to manually apply it before seating the cooler to the CPU.

    4. Installing the RAM

      1. Look for the RAM slots in your motherboard & determine the number of slots (2 or 4).
      2. If you are going to fill all the slots, simply lift the tabs on the side and snap the RAM sticks into place.
      3. Otherwise, consult the user manual for the correct configuration and populate the slots accordingly.

    5. Installing the power supply

      1. Find the slot where the power supply would go. Usually on the bottom side of the case.
      2. Look for the perforated side of the slot and align it with the fan on the PSU when installing it.
      3. If you're using a non-modular or semi-modular power supply, now is the time to run the attached cables
        through the case to where they'll need to end up

    6. Installing the motherboard

      1. If your motherboard came with an attatched IO shield, (a rectangular sheet of metal with cutout for the motherboard's ports) snap it to the cutout. Be sure to orient it correctly.
      2. Before installing the motherboard, make sure the wires are routed correctly.
      3. After that, screw in the motherboard. Refer to the user manual for the position of the screws relative to the case.

    7. Installing storage

      1. Same as the PSU, you can find slots or stack bays for the storage in the case. They may have little plastic switches,
        in which case they are tool-free bays, or they may just look like metal brackets.
      2. If you have tool-free bays, each bay will have its own plastic lever or switch. Open or unlock the lever or switch and
        you should be able to pull out the tray.
      3. Place the drive and screw it in.
      4. If you don't have tool-free bays, you'll see a metal bracket (it will be big, like a sheet), with slats or holes in it.
      5. To put a drive in one of these "bays," all you have to do is slide the drive between the metal bracket and the side of your
        case and screw it into place.
      6. Once your drives are all in place, connect them to the motherboard (using a SATA cable, which should have come with either
        your drive or your motherboard) and to the power supply.

    8. Installing operating system

      1. Before installing the operating system make sure the the pc has no errors turning on and all the components are connected properly.
      2. Plug in the USB flash drive that contains your OS, as well as a monitor, mouse, and keyboard, and turn on your PC.
      3. First, you'll want to check to makes sure your components are all installed and being recognized. Find the page in BIOS that
        shows your PC's system info (different motherboards have different BIOS setups, but you should be able to find a
        screen that gives you this information) and check to make sure the system is recognizing everything you've installed so far.
      4. Next, navigate the BIOS until you find the Boot page (may be called "Boot Order" or "Boot Priority"). Change the boot order so
        that your flash drive is first and the drive you want to install your OS on (if you're using an SSD as a boot drive,
        you will want to install the OS here) is second.
      5. Restart your computer. Your computer will boot from the USB and the OS installer will pop up. Follow the instructions to finish
        the installation.

Now we are done assembling the computer. Remember that this guide is for basic computers only. This is not 100% accurate and it is reccomended
that you follow the instructions on the manuals that came with the components.
